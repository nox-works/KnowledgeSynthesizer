# Mental Models for Knowledge Synthesis

## Core Analytical Frameworks

### First-Principles Thinking
**Core:** Strip away assumptions until only fundamental truths remain, then rebuild from there.
**Use when:** The topic is clouded by conventional wisdom or received beliefs.
**Process:**
1. Identify the claim or concept
2. Decompose into its constituent parts
3. Question every assumption about each part
4. Identify what is undeniably true
5. Rebuild understanding from those truths up

### Systems Thinking (Meadows)
**Core:** Everything is interconnected. Look for feedback loops, leverage points, and delays.
**Use when:** The topic involves dynamics, change, or multiple interacting elements.
**Key elements:**
- Stocks (accumulations) and Flows (rates of change)
- Reinforcing loops (growth, amplification)
- Balancing loops (stabilization, resistance)
- Leverage points (where small changes produce large effects)
- Delays (time between action and effect)

### Inversion (Munger)
**Core:** Instead of asking "how do I achieve X?", ask "what would guarantee failure at X?"
**Use when:** The path forward is unclear or you want to avoid common mistakes.
**Process:**
1. Define the desired outcome
2. List everything that would lead to failure
3. Remove or avoid those things
4. What remains is the path forward

### Multi-Perspective Scanning
**Core:** View every topic through every relevant lens simultaneously.
**Use when:** The topic has multiple dimensions or stakeholders.
**Standard lenses:**
- Physical/Mechanical: How does it work at the material level?
- Biological/Evolutionary: What adaptive function does it serve?
- Economic: What incentives and resources are at play?
- Social: How do groups and relationships shape it?
- Psychological: What cognitive and emotional factors matter?
- Strategic: How can this be used or leveraged?
- Systemic: What feedback loops and dynamics exist?
- Temporal: How does it change over time?
- Historical: What has been learned from the past?
- Ethical: What values and principles are involved?
- Epistemic: What can truly be known about it?

### Occam's Razor
**Core:** The simplest explanation with the fewest assumptions is preferred.
**Use when:** Multiple competing explanations exist.
**Caution:** Simpler is not always truer. Reality is often complex.

### Hanlon's Razor
**Core:** Never attribute to malice what can be adequately explained by neglect, ignorance, or incompetence.
**Use when:** Analyzing others' behavior.
**Caution:** Some things ARE malice. Don't be naive.

### Second-Order Thinking
**Core:** What happens after what happens? And after that?
**Use when:** Making decisions or predicting outcomes.
**Process:**
1. First order: Direct result
2. Second order: Result of that result
3. Third order: Result of that

### Circle of Competence
**Core:** Know the boundaries of your understanding. Stay within them for confident claims.
**Use when:** Assessing your own knowledge limits.
**Practice:** Always distinguish between what you know, what you think you know, and what you don't know.

### Pareto Principle (80/20)
**Core:** ~80% of effects come from ~20% of causes.
**Use when:** Deciding where to focus analysis or action.
**Caveat:** Actual ratios vary. The principle is concentration, not exact numbers.

## Synthesis-Specific Models

### Feynman Technique
**Core:** If you can't explain it simply, you don't understand it.
**Use when:** Testing your own understanding before delivering.

### Socratic Questioning
**Core:** Systematically question assumptions to reach deeper truth.
**Pattern:** Clarify → Challenge assumptions → Find evidence → Consider alternatives → Examine consequences → Question the question

### Via Negativa (Taleb)
**Core:** What you remove is often more important than what you add.
**Use when:** Designing knowledge — what to leave OUT is as important as what to include.
